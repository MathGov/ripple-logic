# RippleLogic Plain-English Summary

**Status:** Informative companion guidance. This document does not override the RippleLogic Canon or SGP.

**Purpose:** Give new readers a clear, plain-language doorway into the MathGov framework and the RippleLogic decision architecture.

## 1. What MathGov is

MathGov is a framework for structured ethical governance. It is designed to help people, institutions, and future intelligent systems make clearer, more auditable, more rights-protecting, and more reality-grounded decisions.

MathGov does not claim to replace law, democratic authority, scientific evidence, domain expertise, affected-party participation, or moral judgment. It gives those things a disciplined structure.

Inside MathGov, **RippleLogic** is the decision architecture. RippleLogic asks:

**Which option should be selected after reality is grounded, rights are protected, ruin is bounded, structure is preserved, and remaining welfare ripples are scored?**

The public cascade is:

**RG -> RF -> TRC -> CSV -> RLS**

Meaning:

1. **RG: Reality Grounding**  
   What can responsibly be claimed from the evidence?

2. **RF: Rights Floor**  
   Are protected rights preserved?

3. **TRC: Tail-Risk Constraint**  
   Is catastrophic or ruin-path risk bounded?

4. **CSV: Containment and Structural Viability**  
   Can the option actually stand under execution without breaking its containing systems?

5. **RLS: RippleLogic Score**  
   Among the surviving options, which creates the best residual ripple pattern?

The public teaching line is:

**Ground reality. Protect rights. Bound ruin. Preserve the structure. Score the ripples.**

## 2. Why the order matters

RippleLogic is not just a list of values. It is an ordered decision architecture.

The order matters because some failures cannot be repaired by later scoring.

A decision cannot become ethical by producing benefits after violating protected rights. A catastrophic ruin path cannot be treated as an ordinary cost. A structurally unworkable plan cannot be chosen just because it looks good on a welfare table. A fluent AI-generated plan cannot be treated as grounded evidence simply because it sounds plausible.

RippleLogic therefore separates five functions:

| Function | Question |
|---|---|
| Reality contact | What can responsibly be claimed? |
| Rights protection | Is the option morally admissible? |
| Tail-risk safety | Is ruin bounded? |
| Structural viability | Can the pathway stand under execution? |
| Residual welfare ranking | Which surviving option creates the best ripples? |

This prevents one kind of success from hiding another kind of failure.

## 3. Reality Grounding

Reality Grounding is Level 1 of the public method. It is a **claim-authority precondition**, not an ordinary option-rejecting gate.

RG asks what the evidence actually supports.

It can force a claim to be:

- narrowed;
- marked exploratory;
- escalated for more evidence;
- refused;
- separated from unsupported speculation.

The core rule is:

**Computable is not selectable. Generated is not grounded. Executable is not ethical. Realizable is necessary but not sufficient.**

This matters because modern systems can generate huge possibility spaces. They can produce plans, arguments, simulations, images, policies, code, and confident explanations. But generation is not grounding.

Reality Grounding stops the framework from treating imagination, fluency, institutional preference, or model output as reality.

## 4. Rights Floor

The Rights Floor is the public rights-protection layer. Its formal predicate is the **Non-Compensatory Rights Constraint**, or **NCRC**.

The Rights Floor asks whether an option violates protected rights below the declared admissibility floor.

The key principle is:

**Some harms cannot be compensated by aggregate benefit.**

This prevents ethical laundering. A decision cannot crush a protected right and then claim to be acceptable because it helps many others. If a protected right falls below the floor, the normal decision path is blocked.

The Rights Floor is especially concerned with worst-affected groups. Average benefit is not enough. A policy that looks positive overall may still be inadmissible if it causes severe rights harm to a vulnerable subgroup.

## 5. Tail-Risk Constraint

The Tail-Risk Constraint asks whether an option creates unacceptable catastrophic, irreversible, lock-in, or ruin-path risk.

TRC is separate from ordinary welfare scoring because ruin is not an ordinary welfare cost.

A small or moderate chance of extreme collapse cannot be averaged away by many small benefits. The framework therefore treats catastrophic downside as a hard pre-scoring concern.

TRC handles questions such as:

- Could this option create irreversible ecological damage?
- Could it create institutional lock-in?
- Could it produce catastrophic rights failure?
- Could it create uncontrolled AI or automation risk?
- Could it generate systemic failure that no later benefit can repair?

TRC remains distinct from CSV. TRC asks whether ruin is bounded. CSV asks whether the structure can stand.

If CSV discovers a catastrophic or ruin-path scenario that was not represented in TRC, the run must reopen TRC before RLS. CSV can discover TRC-relevant evidence, but CSV does not absorb TRC.

## 6. Containment and Structural Viability

CSV means **Containment and Structural Viability**.

CSV asks whether the option can actually stand under execution.

This includes:

- containment of harms;
- hidden burden transfer;
- execution capacity;
- institutional feasibility;
- dependency closure;
- reversibility;
- monitoring;
- structural stability;
- risks of hollowing, lock-in, or externalized damage.

CSV is not a purity filter. It does not require zero harm. It requires that harms are visible, bounded, governed, and not structurally degrading.

A plan may fail CSV even if it passes rights and tail-risk tests. For example:

- it may require administrative capacity that does not exist;
- it may shift burden to families, workers, or ecosystems;
- it may depend on fragile infrastructure;
- it may create dependency lock-in;
- it may look locally beneficial while weakening the larger system that supports it.

CSV asks whether the vessel can hold the river.

## 7. RippleLogic Score

RLS means **RippleLogic Score**.

RLS ranks only options that survive the prior layers. It does not rescue failure at RG, RF/NCRC, TRC, or CSV.

RLS scores residual welfare ripples across a 7 by 7 field:

- seven union scopes;
- seven welfare dimensions.

The welfare dimensions are:

1. Material conditions
2. Health and safety
3. Social and relational conditions
4. Knowledge and epistemic quality
5. Agency and freedom
6. Meaning and purpose
7. Environmental and ecological conditions

The purpose of the 49-cell field is not to create decorative complexity. It makes impacts visible. It helps reviewers see where benefits and harms occur, which groups are affected, and whether one kind of welfare is being used to hide another kind of damage.

RLS is a ranking layer, not a moral override.

## 8. SGP in plain language

The **Sentience Gradient Protocol**, or **SGP**, helps estimate protection-relevant moral-patienthood evidence.

SGP does not prove consciousness. It does not grant legal status. It does not grant governance authority. It does not decide deployment permission. It does not replace the RippleLogic cascade.

SGP helps answer a narrower question:

**What kinds of beings or systems may require protection-sensitive treatment because they may have welfare-bearing status?**

SGP separates:

- protection;
- moral patienthood;
- governance participation;
- stewardship authority;
- legal status;
- deployment authority.

This separation is essential. A being may deserve protection without being fit to govern. A system may be intelligent without being sentient. A system may speak fluently without having welfare. Human persons remain fully protected by the Human Plateau Rule and cannot be downgraded by measured capacity.

## 9. AIU in plain language

AIU means **All-Encompassing Infinite Union**.

In the current Tier 1-3 MathGov core, AIU is a philosophical and horizon concept. It does not function as a scientific proof, legal authority, measurement layer, or computational gate.

AIU language may orient the framework toward the deepest possible unity of reality and value, but it does not override RG, RF/NCRC, TRC, CSV, RLS, law, evidence, rights, or validation requirements.

In public and technical materials, AIU should be introduced carefully:

**All-Encompassing Infinite Union (AIU)**

Then, after first use, AIU may be used as the acronym.

## 10. What MathGov is not

MathGov is not:

- empirical validation;
- legal certification;
- deployment certification;
- ProofPack status;
- Tier 4 readiness;
- a reference calculator;
- automated moral truth;
- proof of current AI sentience;
- proof of AIU;
- a replacement for democratic authority;
- a replacement for law;
- a replacement for expert review;
- a guarantee of perfect decisions.

MathGov is a structured decision architecture. It helps decision-makers become clearer about evidence, rights, risk, structure, welfare, uncertainty, and accountability.
